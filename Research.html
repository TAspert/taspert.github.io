Overview

During my (short) carrer, I developed several tools to study cells at the single-cell level, mainly using timelapse microscopy. In this section, I will briefly explain the major developmental project I have ran, and the biological questions I have been able to address using these tools.

Development of microfluidic devices for single cell dynamics assays

My research greatly rely on the development of custom-made microfluidic devices to monitor the growth of individual cells over multi-generation or several days timescales. For that, I have set up a clean room that allowed me to perform the standard SU8-based photolithography processes in order to generate microfluidic prototypes. More details about my designs

Schematic of one microfluidic device developped for replicative aging experiments

Automated longitudinal analysis of single-cell dynamics

A typical microfluidics timelapse experiment allows to monitor 400 000 cell divisions. The manual analysis of this extensive quantity of data is tedious and time-consuming.

Therefore, we developped and trained deep-learning based networks along with a software, DetecDiv, to automate and parallelize the detection of cell divisions, the volume and the fluorescence of single cells. See more details.

Dynamics and mechanism of entry into replicative senescence in yeast

A mother cell divides assymetrically a certain number of times before dying. It accumulates aging factors thoughout its lifespan but daughter cells are born rejuvenated and free of these factors.

Budding yeast cells undergo a limited number of divisions before entering senescence and eventually dying, a phenomenon known as replicative aging. Over the last twenty years, classical genetic analyses have identified genes and pathways that regulate this process, yet a detailed understanding of the cascade of events driving the entry into senescence is largely missing. In this context, a few groups have pioneered the development of microfluidics-based techniques to trap individual mother cells and to monitor their successive divisions from birth to death under the microscope, in the past 10 years. Using this methodology, it has be shown that cells undergo an abrupt transition (referred to as the Senescence Entry Point, or SEP) to a slow division regime that precedes cell death.[Ferhmann et al. 2013]

Graphical display of cell trajectories after alignment from cell birth (left) or from Senescence Entry Point (right). Each horizontal line corresponds to a single mother cell, and each segment along the line indicates one cell cycle. The color-coding shows the duration of the cell cycle.

Following up on their observations, the Charvin group has recently shown that the SEP is mechanistically associated with the accumulation of extrachromosomal rDNA circles (ERCs), a long described hallmark of aging in yeast. [Morlot et al. 2018].

Schematic showing the three steps involved in ERC-dependent senescence (ERC excision, ERC self-replication, and post-SEP interval).

Based on this, I am now trying to understand 1) what are the statistics of the first ERC excision..... and 2) how these ERC are toxic to the cell.

Dynamics of response to starvation

Quiescence is a highly conserved phenomenon of living organisms. This defense mechanism allows unicellulars to survive over long time scales under unfavorable conditions. However, the mechanisms allowing the transition between proliferative state and quiescence are still poorly understood, in part because they have not been studied under physiologically relevant conditions. In the case of microorganisms, indeed, this requires observing the cells under unperturbed conditions as they progressively modify their growth environment. The second difficulty is that quiescence is systematically associated with a high degree of cellular heterogeneity, the emergence dynamics of which is unknown, making the analysis very complex. Our study overcomes these barriers by proposing a new methodology based on microfluidics that allows us to observe the metabolic transitions in individual cells from fermentation to stationary phase in real-time. This technique offers a combined approach that respects the temporality of medium changes dictated by population growth while allowing single-cell level observations.
